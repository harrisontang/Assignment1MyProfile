Project Idea

Gamify fresh food and healthy eating by focusing on education, discovery, data, and social media, while simultaneously disrupting the fresh foods market and evening the playing field.

More

Overview

A smartphone application that identifies various fruits and vegetables and tracks their consumption into personalised data analytics over days, weeks, and months. By photographing their produce discoveries, users can view descriptions, nutritional values, recipe suggestions and share on social media.
Progressively filling the produce catalogue will connect local grocers and suppliers with new customers, encouraging users to travel and experiment with unfamiliar fruits and vegetables, rewarding them with vouchers and promotions and thus improving Australian diets through variety and discovery.


Motivation

Despite being one of the most food-secure nations in the world, many Australians struggle maintaining a recommended diet of two servings of fruit and five servings of vegetables per day.
Reports from a 2016 CSIRO study into Australian fruit and vegetable consumption showed 51 percent of adults are not eating the recommended daily intake of fruit, while 66 percent are not eating enough vegetables, citing improved variety and economic accessibility as possible solutions. A 2018 Roy Morgan report into total grocery market share showed non-Big Four supermarkets held 9.1% of market share, while the Big Four (Woolworths Group, Coles Group, Aldi and IGA) made up a combined 80.1% of market share.
This means approximately one in ten consumers are shopping at local grocers and have access to wider variety. By gamifying healthy eating habits, consumers can fulfil their dietary requirements with progression, rewards, and interaction (much like how applications like Duolingo and Fitbit gamifying language education and fitness respectively).
While there are already plenty of applications that focus on dietary consumption and food-journaling, the aim of developing this app is to improve fruit and vegetable intake by gamifying the experience of discovering, sharing and consuming fresh produce.

Next

Description

On the consumer level, the application provides customers with product information through interaction. It features a catalogue of silhouetted fruit and vegetable varieties for users to progressively complete and experiment with (with expansions of the catalogue through regular updates and fixes).
When a user discovers and consumes a particular produce, they will photograph and feed the image through the app, which will return specific details on the variety including type, nutritional value, and price comparisons between available and stocked suppliers.
Logging a new fruit or vegetable will fill the relative space in the catalogue and unlock recipe suggestions, while registering a previously logged produce will add “experience”. Over time, gaining experience with a particular produce will unlock further recipes and promotions on the application.
These interactions are logged onto a food diary and can be optionally shared across social medias, providing users recommendations and analytics into their fruit and vegetable eating habits across user-defined stretches of time.

Through this discovery, users can “follow” a favourite supermarket or grocer to receive updates on new produce, current stock levels, and specials.
On the supply level, smaller fresh food chains and retailers will reach out to potential new customers by showcasing greater variety in locally stocked fruit and vegetables through the application, while larger chains continue to hold the retailer price advantage.
Retailers will receive more targeted analytics on sales and interest through social media interaction and data collection. Such an application would also be useful to farmers and horticulturists to accurately identify differences in crop. There is also latent potential to connect with new farmers and suppliers as the userbase grows.

Expansion into other countries and territories will provide users with an even larger catalogue to complete. Much like popular mobile apps like Pokemon GO, travelling across states and countries will be required to fill the fresh food catalogue, as certain produce can only be purchased and enjoyed in specific regions.

Tools and Technologies

The application will be available on both Android and iOS, utilising both camera functionality and GPS, with access to a cloud-based database which holds relevant information on foods, userbase and market trends.
Like the popular plant identifying app, Plant Snap, the application will require a unique algorithm which identifies common to rare kinds of fruit and vegetables through camera sensors and organises user-submitted data into a relevant fashion (aligning with consumption analytics, journaling and market trends), thus building the database for users to draw from.
Common languages used in app development include JavaScript, Kotlin and C++, with the latter being the preferred choice for its compatibility with cloud computing apps.
Presently, app development kits such as Google’s Flutter are becoming more accessible to retail consumers and present a compelling, cost-effective means for app production. Flutter is an open-source project and combines Dart programming language with C and C++.


Skills Required

This project will primarily require skills in programming, marketing, and an understanding of the fresh foods retail space. Programmers adept in Dart with a familiarity to C++ programming languages will be required, as will experience in cloud-storage and networking. As this will be a collaborative project, strong communication between programmers and an understanding of GitHub repositories will also be beneficial.
A marketing and sales team to engage with large and local businesses will be necessary to form a client base for providing product information to customers.

Outcome

The success of this application will be based on user and business engagement, as the relationship between food, business and customer is the engine behind this app.
With its universal and disruptive approach to fresh foods, it has appeal-potential for a wide and consumer-conscious userbase. Farmers, horticulturalists, and nutritionists will also find value in the application.
By putting more power into the consumer’s hands, they can make more informed decisions – gamifying it can deliver more tangibles for the consumer in progression, discovery, and education.
As Australians improve their fruit and vegetable eating habits, so will their overall health
